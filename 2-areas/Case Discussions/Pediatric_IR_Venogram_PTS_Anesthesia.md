# Anesthesia for Pediatric IR Venogram — Post-Thrombotic Syndrome

**Patient:** 13 y 11 m male | **Diagnosis:** Moderate PTS, right lower extremity | **Procedure:** RLE venogram (IR, Dr. Warren) | **Estimated weight:** ~50 kg (adjust all dosing to actual weight)

---

## Anesthesia Technique Selection

This is a **diagnostic IR venogram** — relatively short, minimally painful (IV contrast injection, femoral venous access), requiring **immobility** for fluoroscopic imaging.

**Recommended approach: Deep sedation / MAC with spontaneous ventilation.**

- GA with ETT is generally unnecessary for a cooperative 13-year-old undergoing a diagnostic venogram.
- If the patient is anxious or uncooperative, convert to GA with LMA.
- Discuss with IR team: expected duration, need for breath-holds, planned venous access site (femoral vs popliteal).

---

## Preop Considerations Specific to This Case

**Anticoagulation status — CRITICAL:**
- PTS with history of DVT → likely on anticoagulants (warfarin, LMWH, or DOAC).
- Confirm last dose timing and current INR/anti-Xa level BEFORE proceeding.
- If on warfarin: INR must be checked day-of. IR team may require INR < 1.5 for femoral access.
- If on LMWH: last dose should be ≥12 h (prophylactic) or ≥24 h (therapeutic) prior.
- Coordinate with hematology and IR regarding bridging strategy if anticoagulation was held.

**Vascular access:**
- Right leg has PTS — avoid right lower extremity IV sites if possible (compromised venous drainage).
- Place IV in left upper extremity.
- The IR team will obtain femoral venous access for the venogram; discuss if they want you to avoid specific sites.

**Contrast allergy:** Confirm no prior contrast reactions. If history of reaction → premedicate (see below).

## Intraop Anesthesia Plan

### Sedation Protocol (MAC / Deep Sedation)

**Option A — Propofol-based (preferred for short diagnostic procedure):**

| Step | Drug | Dose |
|------|------|------|
| Induction | Propofol | 1–2 mg/kg IV (50–100 mg) titrated slowly |
| Maintenance | Propofol infusion | 100–200 mcg/kg/min (start low, titrate to effect) |
| Analgesia | Fentanyl | 0.5–1 mcg/kg (25–50 mcg) at femoral access |
| Rescue antiemetic | Ondansetron | 0.15 mg/kg IV (max 4 mg) |

- This is a diagnostic venogram — stimulation is brief (puncture site + contrast injection).
- Propofol infusion at 100–150 mcg/kg/min is usually sufficient once access is established.
- Titrate to maintain spontaneous ventilation and immobility.

**Option B — Propofol + Dexmedetomidine (if longer procedure anticipated):**

| Step | Drug | Dose |
|------|------|------|
| Dexmedetomidine load | Dexmedetomidine | 0.5–1 mcg/kg IV over 10 min |
| Maintenance | Dexmedetomidine infusion | 0.5–1 mcg/kg/hr |
| Supplemental sedation | Propofol boluses | 0.5 mg/kg PRN for stimulating moments |
| Analgesia | Fentanyl | 0.5–1 mcg/kg at femoral access |

- Dexmedetomidine preserves respiratory drive — advantage in IR suite with limited airway access.
- Expect bradycardia (HR may drop to 50s–60s in adolescents) — generally well tolerated.
- If treating dex-induced bradycardia with glycopyrrolate, use lower doses (2–5 mcg/kg) to avoid rebound hypertension.

**Option C — GA with LMA (if uncooperative or procedure upgraded to intervention):**

| Step | Drug | Dose |
|------|------|------|
| Induction | Propofol | 2–3 mg/kg IV |
| LMA | Size 4 LMA | |
| Maintenance | Sevoflurane 1–2% in O2/air OR propofol TIVA 150–250 mcg/kg/min | |
| Analgesia | Fentanyl | 1 mcg/kg |
| Paralysis | Generally NOT needed for venogram | |

### Monitoring

| Monitor | Notes |
|---------|-------|
| Standard ASA monitors | ECG, SpO2, NIBP, EtCO2 |
| **Capnography (EtCO2)** | **Mandatory** — nasal cannula with EtCO2 sampling if MAC; sidestream from LMA if GA. This is the single most important monitor for deep sedation in remote locations. |
| Temperature | If procedure > 30 min |
| Processed EEG (BIS) | Optional — may help titrate propofol in obese or complicated patients |

**Monitor placement:** Place monitors and IV access on the LEFT side (opposite to procedure leg). Ensure all cables/lines route away from the fluoroscopy field.

### Airway Management

- Position: Supine on IR table (narrow, limited padding — add padding as needed).
- Nasal cannula with EtCO2 + supplemental O2 (3–5 L/min) for MAC.
- Have oral airway, LMA size 4, and suction immediately available in the IR suite.
- **Jaw thrust** is your primary rescue maneuver for propofol-induced obstruction.
- Full difficult airway cart should be accessible (not necessarily in the room, but on the same floor with rapid retrieval).

## Anticoagulation During the Procedure

The IR team may administer **intraprocedural heparin** to prevent catheter-related thrombosis:

- **Typical dose:** 50–100 units/kg IV bolus (2,500–5,000 units for 50 kg patient).
- **ACT target:** ~200–250 seconds if systemic heparinization is needed.
- **Be aware:** If the patient is already on background anticoagulation (even if held), the effect of procedural heparin may be augmented.
- Protamine should be available for reversal if bleeding occurs: **1 mg protamine per 100 units heparin** given in the last 2–3 hours.

## IR Suite-Specific Concerns

### Radiation Safety (ALARA Principles)
- Wear **wrap-around lead apron** (0.5 mm Pb equivalent) + **thyroid shield** + **leaded glasses**.
- Maximize distance from the fluoroscopy source — the inverse-square law means doubling your distance reduces exposure by 75%.
- **6 feet of air ≈ 0.5 mm lead** (>99% beam attenuation). Stay as far back as possible during imaging runs.
- Use portable lead screens between you and the C-arm when feasible.
- Wear a radiation dosimetry badge.
- Anesthesia personnel receive up to **3–4× more radiation** than the proceduralist due to room positioning — be proactive about shielding.

### Contrast Reactions
Iodinated contrast will be injected for the venogram. Be prepared for:

| Reaction Severity | Signs | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Mild | Urticaria, pruritus, warmth, nausea | Diphenhydramine 1 mg/kg IV (max 50 mg) |
| Moderate | Bronchospasm, facial edema, hypotension | Epinephrine 1–5 mcg/kg IV, albuterol, fluids |
| Severe / Anaphylaxis | Cardiovascular collapse, severe bronchospasm | Epinephrine 10 mcg/kg IV, aggressive fluid resuscitation, call for help |

**If known prior contrast reaction:** Premedicate with:
- Methylprednisolone 1 mg/kg IV (or prednisone 0.5 mg/kg PO) 12 h and 2 h before
- Diphenhydramine 1 mg/kg PO/IV 1 h before

### Access and Positioning
- IR tables are **narrow and hard** — add gel padding, secure the patient, arm boards for IV access arm.
- The **C-arm** will swing around the right leg — keep your anesthesia workspace (machine, monitors, drugs) at the **head/left side**.
- Ensure long IV extension tubing so you can reach IV pumps from behind the lead shield.
- Warming blanket on upper body (patient exposed during leg imaging).

## Emergency Scenarios

### 1. Vessel Perforation / Hemorrhage
- **Signs:** Sudden hypotension, tachycardia, leg swelling, IR team alerts you.
- **Response:** Large-bore IV access (if not already), aggressive crystalloid/colloid bolus (20 mL/kg), type and screen should be sent pre-procedure. Call for blood products if significant. Convert to GA if not already. Vasopressors: phenylephrine 1–2 mcg/kg or ephedrine 0.1–0.2 mg/kg.

### 2. Contrast Anaphylaxis
- See table above. Have **epinephrine drawn up** (10 mcg/mL dilution: 1 mg in 100 mL = 10 mcg/mL).

### 3. Pulmonary Embolism (PE)
- This patient has PTS from prior DVT — manipulation of thrombosed/stenotic veins carries risk of clot dislodgement.
- **Signs:** Sudden desaturation, hypotension, tachycardia, RV strain on echo, increased EtCO2 dead space (sudden drop in EtCO2 with rising PaCO2).
- **Response:** 100% O2, intubate, hemodynamic support with vasopressors (norepinephrine or epinephrine infusion), IV heparin bolus (80 units/kg) if not already anticoagulated, call for emergent CT angiography or IR-directed catheter thrombectomy/thrombolysis. Consider tPA if massive PE with cardiovascular collapse (alteplase 0.1 mg/kg over 15 min — discuss with team).

### 4. Airway Obstruction During Sedation
- Jaw thrust → oral airway → LMA → intubate if needed.
- **Challenge in IR:** The C-arm may obstruct access to the head. Communicate with IR to swing the C-arm away immediately if airway emergency occurs. **Pre-plan this with the IR team before starting.**

### 5. Bradycardia (Dexmedetomidine or Vasovagal)
- Glycopyrrolate 5–10 mcg/kg IV (use lower end if on dexmedetomidine).
- Atropine 0.02 mg/kg IV if symptomatic/severe.
- Stop dexmedetomidine infusion.
