# Anesthesia Plan: Henry Horvath (8y2m, Male)

## Diagnosis & Procedures
- **Dx:** Chronic extrahepatic portal vein thrombosis → portal hypertension, cavernous transformation of the portal vein (CTPV), **grade 3 esophageal varices**, thrombocytopenia, hypersplenism
- **Surgical history:** EGD with variceal banding on 3/28/26 (17 days prior) — healing banding ulcers likely present
- **Recent workup:** Multiple admissions for MR imaging and endoscopic evaluation
- **Procedures (4/14):**
  1. EGD with repeat variceal banding (GI)
  2. IR portography with wedge pressures
  3. Percutaneous liver biopsy (IR)

---

## Pre-Anesthetic Assessment — Critical Data Needed

### Labs (day-of or within 24h)
- **CBC with differential** — baseline platelet count is the single most important number. Expect significant thrombocytopenia (hypersplenism).
- **Coagulation panel:** PT/INR, PTT, fibrinogen. Hepatic synthetic function may be impaired → coagulopathy beyond just low platelets.
- **Hepatic panel:** albumin (colloid oncotic pressure, drug binding), bilirubin, transaminases.
- **Type and screen** — must be current. Strongly consider **type and crossmatch for 1–2 units pRBC** given variceal banding + liver biopsy in a coagulopathic child.
- **BMP:** electrolytes, glucose, creatinine (hepatorenal considerations).

### Pre-Op Thresholds & Transfusion Plan
- **Platelets ≥50,000** for liver biopsy (many IR teams want ≥70,000). Confirm threshold with IR and GI teams pre-op.
- **INR ≤1.5** for liver biopsy. If elevated, consider vitamin K (0.5 mg/kg IV, max 10 mg) or FFP (10–15 mL/kg).
- **Fibrinogen >150 mg/dL.** If low, cryoprecipitate (1 unit/5 kg).
- Have **platelets, pRBC, and FFP immediately available** — not just on hold, but physically in the room or satellite fridge. This child can bleed fast.

### History to Confirm
- Previous anesthetic history and any complications.
- Current medications (beta-blockers for portal HTN? — common in adults, less so in peds but check).
- History of variceal bleeding episodes.
- Airway history — any prior intubations, airway concerns.
- NPO status (standard pediatric: clears 2h, breast milk 4h, solids 6–8h).
- Ascites? (affects FRC, aspiration risk, abdominal compartment pressure).
- Any known hepatopulmonary syndrome or portopulmonary hypertension (rare in peds but present in chronic portal HTN — SpO2 on room air, any dyspnea/platypnea).

## Anesthesia Concerns — Ranked by Severity

### 1. Hemorrhage (Primary Concern)
- **Variceal bleeding during banding:** Can be sudden and massive. The EGD scope compresses the esophagus and can tamponade, but if a varix ruptures during banding → rapid hemorrhage into the airway/GI tract.
- **Liver biopsy bleeding:** Percutaneous biopsy in a thrombocytopenic, potentially coagulopathic child with portal HTN and collateral vessels is high-risk for subcapsular hematoma, intraperitoneal bleed, or hemobilia.
- **Combined risk is multiplicative** — doing both procedures in one session.

### 2. Shared Airway (EGD)
- The endoscope occupies the esophagus and can compress the trachea/ETT, especially in a child.
- Risk of aspiration of blood or gastric contents.
- Ventilation may be intermittently compromised.

### 3. Coagulopathy
- Thrombocytopenia (hypersplenism — consumptive + sequestration).
- Possible impaired hepatic synthetic function (low factors, low fibrinogen, elevated INR).
- Combined = bleeding diathesis that may not respond to platelets alone.

### 4. Hepatic Dysfunction
- Altered drug metabolism — reduced clearance of many anesthetic agents.
- Low albumin → increased free fraction of protein-bound drugs.
- Potential for hepatorenal syndrome.

### 5. Portal Hypertension Physiology
- Collateral venous circulation (esophageal, abdominal wall, retroperitoneal) — abdominal/thoracic procedures carry higher bleeding risk from unexpected collaterals.
- Possible splenomegaly — avoid left-sided rib/abdominal trauma.
- Possible ascites → reduced FRC, atelectasis, higher aspiration risk.

### 6. Logistical — Multi-Location Anesthesia
- If EGD in GI suite and then transport to IR: transport under GA with full monitoring, secured airway, and resuscitation readiness.
- If both in IR suite: confirm GI team can do EGD/banding in IR and that the room setup supports both.

## Anesthetic Plan

### Monitoring
- **Standard ASA monitors** (ECG, SpO2, NIBP, EtCO2, temp).
- **Arterial line** — strongly recommended given hemorrhage risk during banding + biopsy. Radial preferred. Allows real-time BP monitoring and serial lab draws (ABG, Hgb, coags) without repeated venipuncture.
  - **22G arterial catheter** (right radial preferred).
  - Consider pre-procedural placement under sedation/local if tolerated.
- **Two large-bore peripheral IVs** — largest gauge achievable (20G or better in an 8-year-old). One dedicated to resuscitation access.
- **Temperature monitoring** — esophageal probe (placed after intubation, before EGD scope insertion; remove before EGD and replace after).
- **Foley catheter** — monitor UOP given hemorrhage risk and potentially long combined case.

### IV Access Strategy
- Two peripheral IVs minimum.
- Discuss with team whether a **central line** is warranted. If portography involves catheterization of hepatic veins, IR already has central venous access — coordinate.
- If only peripheral access: ensure fluid warmer + pressure bag setup on at least one line.

### Airway Plan
- **General endotracheal anesthesia (GETA)** — mandatory for shared airway during EGD and for airway protection during banding (blood aspiration risk).
- **Cuffed ETT** — size 6.0 cuffed (age-based: (age/4) + 3.5 = 5.5 uncuffed → 6.0 cuffed is appropriate; verify with leak test).
- **Oral RAE or standard oral ETT** — oral RAE preferred to route the tube away from the midline, giving the endoscopist better access. Confirm with GI which side they prefer the tube secured.
- **Secure the tube meticulously** — the endoscope will push against it; tape must be bombproof. Waterproof tape (Tegaderm over tape) given blood/secretions.
- **Bite block** — placed alongside ETT to protect both tube and endoscope.
- **Have a plan for ETT dislodgement** — the endoscope can push the tube out. Keep laryngoscope and backup ETT immediately available throughout the EGD portion.
- **Suction** — two suction setups: one for the anesthesiologist (oropharyngeal), one for the endoscopist.

### Induction
- **IV induction** (assuming IV in place from pre-procedural workup/admission):
  - **Propofol** 2–3 mg/kg IV (dose on lean body weight if ascites present).
  - **Fentanyl** 1–2 mcg/kg IV for analgesia and to blunt laryngoscopy response.
  - **Rocuronium** 0.6 mg/kg IV for neuromuscular blockade (facilitates intubation and eliminates coughing/bucking during EGD — important to avoid variceal rupture from straining).
  - Avoid succinylcholine (fasciculations → increased intra-abdominal/portal pressure; also avoid if any concern for hyperkalemia from hepatic/renal dysfunction).
- **RSI consideration:** If ascites is significant or recent GI bleed history → modify to RSI with rocuronium 1.2 mg/kg + cricoid pressure. Have sugammadex available (16 mg/kg for immediate reversal if can't intubate).

### Maintenance
- **Sevoflurane in O2/air** — titrate to MAC 1.0–1.2. Sevoflurane is preferred over isoflurane/desflurane as it has less effect on hepatic artery blood flow.
- Alternatively, **TIVA with propofol/remifentanil** if hepatic function is significantly impaired (avoids volatile agent hepatic metabolism concerns). Propofol infusion 100–200 mcg/kg/min, remifentanil 0.05–0.2 mcg/kg/min.
- **Avoid nitrous oxide** — bowel distension interferes with EGD and portography.
- **Muscle relaxation** — maintain throughout EGD (prevent coughing/retching on scope → variceal rupture). Rocuronium redosing 0.15 mg/kg PRN, guided by train-of-four.
- **Ondansetron** 0.15 mg/kg IV (max 4 mg) — antiemetic, give early.
- **Dexamethasone** 0.15 mg/kg IV (max 8 mg) — antiemetic + anti-inflammatory. Give at induction.

### Analgesia
- EGD/banding: minimal somatic pain but visceral discomfort. Fentanyl 1 mcg/kg increments PRN.
- Liver biopsy: moderate pain at biopsy site.
  - **Local anesthetic by IR** at biopsy site (confirm they will infiltrate).
  - **Fentanyl** boluses titrated (total 3–5 mcg/kg over the case).
  - Consider **acetaminophen** 15 mg/kg IV at induction for multimodal analgesia (avoid ketorolac/NSAIDs — thrombocytopenia, bleeding risk, hepatic concerns).

### Positioning
- **Supine** for induction.
- **Left lateral decubitus** for EGD — standard for endoscopy. Secure ETT connections, ensure all lines accessible, padding under dependent ear and axilla.
- **Supine** for IR portography and liver biopsy — will need to reposition between procedures. Coordinate timing.
- Pad all pressure points. Splenomegaly → avoid external compression on LUQ.

## Blood Product & Resuscitation Plan

### Pre-Staged in Room
| Product | Amount | Indication |
|---------|--------|------------|
| pRBC | 2 units crossmatched | Hemorrhage from banding or biopsy |
| Platelets | 1 apheresis unit | Plt <50K or active bleeding |
| FFP | 2 units (10–15 mL/kg/unit) | INR >1.5 or active coagulopathic bleeding |
| Cryoprecipitate | 1 pool (5 units) | Fibrinogen <150 |

### Transfusion Triggers
- **Hgb <7 g/dL** → transfuse pRBC 10 mL/kg.
- **Platelets <50K** (or <75K if actively bleeding) → platelet transfusion.
- **INR >1.5 with bleeding** → FFP 10–15 mL/kg.
- **Fibrinogen <150** → cryo 1 unit/5 kg.

### Massive Transfusion Protocol
- Know the institutional MTP activation number/process.
- Ratio-based resuscitation: 1:1:1 (pRBC:FFP:platelets) if massive hemorrhage.
- **Tranexamic acid (TXA):** 15 mg/kg IV loading dose over 10 min, then 2 mg/kg/hr infusion if massive/ongoing hemorrhage. (Use with caution — portal vein thrombosis history; discuss risk-benefit, but in life-threatening hemorrhage, TXA is indicated.)
- **Calcium:** monitor iCa with transfusion; replace with CaCl2 10 mg/kg (central) or Ca gluconate 30 mg/kg (peripheral) for iCa <1.0 mmol/L.
- **Fluid warmer + rapid infuser** available and primed.

### Estimated Blood Volume
- EBV = 70 mL/kg × weight (kg). For a typical 8-year-old (~25 kg): **EBV ≈ 1750 mL.**
- Maximum allowable blood loss (MABL) to Hgb 7: calculate from starting Hgb.
  - MABL = EBV × (Hgb_start − Hgb_target) / Hgb_start.

## Emergency Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Massive Variceal Hemorrhage During Banding
**Recognition:** Sudden blood in oropharynx/ETT, hemodynamic instability, falling EtCO2 (hypovolemia), rising HR.

**Immediate actions:**
1. Call for help. Activate MTP.
2. Suction airway aggressively — blood around the ETT cuff can still reach the trachea.
3. Position head-down (Trendelenburg) briefly to improve venous return if hypotensive.
4. **Volume resuscitation:** wide-open crystalloid (20 mL/kg bolus NS or LR), then pRBC 10 mL/kg STAT.
5. Endoscopist attempts direct tamponade with scope/balloon.
6. **Epinephrine** if severe hypotension: 1 mcg/kg IV bolus (dilute: 10 mcg/mL concentration), repeat PRN.
7. Vasopressin analog — **octreotide** 1 mcg/kg IV bolus (max 50 mcg), then 1–2 mcg/kg/hr infusion (reduces splanchnic blood flow/portal pressure).
8. If hemorrhage uncontrollable endoscopically → emergent IR for TIPS or surgical consultation for Sugiura procedure / shunt.
9. Serial ABG/Hgb/coags q15 min during active bleeding.

### Scenario 2: Hemorrhage After Liver Biopsy
**Recognition:** Falling Hgb, hemodynamic instability, increasing abdominal distension/pain, free fluid on IR imaging.

**Immediate actions:**
1. Volume resuscitation + MTP activation as above.
2. IR can attempt embolization of bleeding vessel — patient is already in IR suite.
3. If hemodynamically unstable and IR cannot control → emergent surgical consultation.
4. Maintain normothermia, correct coagulopathy aggressively (FFP, platelets, cryo, TXA).
5. Foley to monitor UOP (target >1 mL/kg/hr).

### Scenario 3: Aspiration of Blood/Gastric Contents
**Recognition:** Desaturation, auscultation of crackles/rhonchi, bloody secretions in ETT.

**Immediate actions:**
1. Suction ETT immediately (pass suction catheter to carina).
2. Increase FiO2 to 1.0.
3. Recruitment maneuvers / increase PEEP to 8–10 cmH2O.
4. Bronchoscopy if large-volume aspiration or ETT obstruction with clot.
5. Avoid lavage (no benefit, increases spread).
6. Antibiotics NOT indicated acutely unless clinical deterioration suggests aspiration pneumonia later.

### Scenario 4: ETT Dislodgement During EGD
**Recognition:** Loss of EtCO2 waveform, audible leak, inability to ventilate.

**Immediate actions:**
1. Ask endoscopist to REMOVE SCOPE immediately.
2. Mask ventilate → reintubate. Keep backup ETT and laryngoscope on the bed throughout.
3. If partial dislodgement (tube in supraglottic position) → consider advancing over a bougie under direct laryngoscopy.
4. Do NOT blindly advance the ETT — risk of esophageal intubation.

### Scenario 5: Anaphylaxis (Blood Product Reaction)
**Recognition:** Urticaria, bronchospasm, hypotension, tachycardia during/after transfusion.

**Immediate actions:**
1. STOP transfusion.
2. **Epinephrine** 10 mcg/kg IM (or 1 mcg/kg IV if access is running).
3. Volume bolus 20 mL/kg NS.
4. Diphenhydramine 1 mg/kg IV (max 50 mg).
5. Methylprednisolone 2 mg/kg IV.
6. Albuterol nebulized for bronchospasm.
7. Send transfusion reaction workup (return blood bag to blood bank, draw post-reaction labs).

## Post-Procedure / Emergence Plan

- **Extubation criteria:** Fully reversed (TOF ratio ≥0.9 — use sugammadex 2 mg/kg for rocuronium reversal), awake, following commands, adequate spontaneous ventilation, no ongoing hemorrhage.
- **Extubate in lateral position** (blood/secretion management) or supine with head-up 30° and suction ready.
- Do NOT extubate until hemostasis is confirmed by both GI and IR teams.
- **PACU/ICU disposition:** Given combined procedures and bleeding risk, strongly consider **monitored bed (PICU or step-down)** for at least 12–24 hours post-procedure.
  - Serial Hgb checks: q4h × 3 post-biopsy.
  - Serial abdominal exams for biopsy-site bleeding.
  - NPO until fully awake, then clear liquids.
- **Pain management post-procedure:** acetaminophen 15 mg/kg PO/IV q6h. Avoid NSAIDs. Low-dose opioid PRN (morphine 0.05 mg/kg IV q3h PRN) for biopsy site pain.
- **Activity:** bed rest × 4–6 hours post liver biopsy (institutional protocol), right lateral decubitus position to tamponade biopsy tract.

## Key Decision Points Summary

1. **Arterial line: yes or no?** — Recommend yes. Hemorrhage risk is high from two independent sources.
2. **RSI vs. standard induction?** — Depends on ascites and recent GI bleeding. If ascites present → RSI.
3. **TXA in portal vein thrombosis?** — Risk-benefit discussion. In life-threatening hemorrhage, use it. Prophylactically, avoid.
4. **Procedure location logistics** — Push for both procedures in IR suite if feasible to avoid intubated transport.
5. **Post-op disposition** — PICU recommended given dual bleeding risk sources.
