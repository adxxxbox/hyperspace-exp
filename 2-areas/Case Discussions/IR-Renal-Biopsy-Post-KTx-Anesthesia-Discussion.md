# Anesthesia for IR US-Guided Transplant Renal Biopsy + Perinephric Collection Aspiration/Drainage

**Patient:** 15 yo F | ESRD (unknown etiology) | S/P DDKT + R nephrectomy (3/28) | POD ~17
**Procedure:** IR US-guided right transplant kidney biopsy ± aspiration/drainage of perinephric hematoma/collection
**Location:** Interventional Radiology suite (non-OR)
**Date planned:** 4/14

---

## Case Summary

Adolescent female with ESRD of unknown etiology, status post deceased donor kidney transplant and right native nephrectomy approximately 2.5 weeks ago. She has persistent elevated creatinine and a perinephric hematoma/collection. Plavix was held 5 days ago per SIR guidelines. The plan is for US-guided transplant kidney biopsy tomorrow with attempted aspiration vs drain placement of the perinephric fluid collection.

---

## Key Anesthesia Concerns

### 1. Post-Transplant Physiology & Immunosuppression

This patient is in the early post-transplant period (~2.5 weeks). Critical considerations:

- **Immunosuppression drug effects:** She is likely on tacrolimus (or cyclosporine), mycophenolate, and steroids. Tacrolimus/cyclosporine cause nephrotoxicity, hypertension, tremor, and electrolyte disturbances (hyperkalemia, hypomagnesemia). Mycophenolate causes bone marrow suppression. Steroids cause hyperglycemia and adrenal suppression.
- **Graft function:** Creatinine is persistently elevated — the biopsy is being done precisely to evaluate this. The graft may be in a state of delayed graft function, rejection, or ATN. Drug dosing must account for impaired renal clearance.
- **Infection risk:** Immunosuppressed patient — strict aseptic technique for all IV access. Fungal and bacterial infections are most common in the first 30 days post-transplant.
- **Stress-dose steroids:** If on chronic steroids (which she almost certainly is post-transplant), consider stress-dose hydrocortisone (50 mg IV for a minor procedure in an adolescent, or 1–2 mg/kg). However, for a brief IR procedure, her usual dose of steroids may suffice — confirm with the transplant team whether she needs supplementation.

### 2. Residual ESRD Physiology

Even post-transplant with a functioning (albeit impaired) graft, ESRD-associated changes persist in the early period:

- **Anemia:** Hgb likely 6–9 g/dL pre-transplant; may still be low post-transplant due to immunosuppressant bone marrow toxicity (azathioprine, mycophenolate) and decreased erythropoietin production from the impaired graft. Check pre-procedure H/H.
- **Electrolytes:** Check K⁺ (risk of hyperkalemia from impaired graft function, tacrolimus, metabolic acidosis), Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, phosphate, bicarbonate. Hyperkalemia lowers resting membrane potential of myocardial cells — if ECG shows peaked T waves, treatment is needed before proceeding.
- **Metabolic acidosis:** Failing/impaired graft cannot excrete adequate hydrogen ions. Acidosis exacerbates hyperkalemia.
- **Platelet dysfunction:** Uremic platelet dysfunction is common in ESRD patients. Even with normal platelet count, function may be impaired. This is particularly relevant given the biopsy is a high-bleeding-risk procedure (per Miller: kidney biopsy is classified as high bleeding risk). DDAVP (0.3 mcg/kg IV over 15–30 min) can be considered if there is clinical concern for uremic bleeding.
- **Hypertension:** Common in ESRD and exacerbated by calcineurin inhibitors. Assess baseline BP and current antihypertensive regimen.
- **Volume status:** Assess carefully — these patients may be either volume overloaded (impaired graft) or depleted (NPO status, diuretics). Clinical signs under anesthesia are unreliable; look at trends in weight, I/Os, and recent labs.

### 3. Coagulation & Bleeding Risk

**This is the single most critical concern for this case.**

- **Kidney biopsy = HIGH bleeding risk procedure** per SIR classification.
- **Plavix (clopidogrel):** Held for 5 days — this meets the SIR guideline recommendation (hold 5 days for high-risk procedures). Confirm no doses were given inadvertently.
- **Coagulation screening (ordered):**
  - Goal INR < 1.5
  - Platelet count > 50,000 (ideally > 80,000 for renal biopsy)
  - Check aPTT
  - Consider checking fibrinogen
- **Uremic platelet dysfunction:** As above, even with normal coags, platelet function may be impaired. Consider:
  - **DDAVP 0.3 mcg/kg IV** (infuse over 15–30 min, give 30–60 min before procedure) — improves platelet adhesion by releasing vWF multimers
  - Ensure Hct > 25–30% (red cells marginate platelets toward vessel walls, improving hemostasis)
- **Post-procedure bleeding:** The perinephric hematoma is already present — adding a biopsy tract increases bleeding risk. Post-biopsy, patient must lie flat 4–6 hours with serial H/H checks at 4 hours.
- **Type and screen** should be current and blood available.

### 4. Non-OR Location (IR Suite)

Per Coté and Miller, anesthesia outside the OR requires the same level of vigilance and monitoring as in the OR:

- **Equipment:** Confirm availability of anesthesia machine (or TIVA capability), suction, airway equipment, full monitoring, emergency drugs, defibrillator, and code cart in the IR suite. MH kit location should be known.
- **Remote location risks:** Delayed access to additional help, limited space, unfamiliar environment for anesthesia team.
- **Radiation/imaging:** Ensure anesthesia team wears lead shielding if fluoroscopy is used (though this is US-guided).
- **Communication:** Establish clear communication with IR team regarding patient positioning, draping access to airway and IV, and anticipated duration.

## Anesthesia Plan

### Pre-Procedure

| Item | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **NPO status** | NPO after midnight (or 8 hrs solids, 6 hrs light meal, 2 hrs clear liquids per ASA guidelines). Confirm NPO compliance. |
| **Pre-procedure labs** | CBC with differential, BMP (K⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺, BUN/Cr, glucose), coagulation panel (PT/INR, aPTT), fibrinogen. Confirm INR < 1.5. |
| **Medications** | Continue immunosuppressants per transplant team (may give with sip of water). Hold any remaining nephrotoxic agents. Confirm Plavix held × 5 days. Consider stress-dose steroids if indicated. |
| **Blood products** | Type and screen current. Have 1 unit pRBC available. |
| **Consent** | Parents must be present or reachable by phone. |
| **DDAVP** | Consider 0.3 mcg/kg IV if concern for uremic platelet dysfunction — give 30–60 min pre-procedure. |
| **Anxiolysis** | Midazolam 0.05 mg/kg IV (max 2 mg) if needed — she is 15 and likely anxious after multiple procedures. Titrate carefully given renal impairment. |

### Anesthetic Technique

**General anesthesia (GA) with LMA or ETT** is preferred over deep sedation for this case because:

1. The patient must remain absolutely still during renal biopsy — any movement risks laceration of the transplant kidney.
2. Breath-holding may be required during the biopsy needle passes.
3. A 15-year-old post-surgical patient with anxiety benefits from controlled GA over unreliable deep sedation.
4. GA allows controlled ventilation and breath-holds on command from IR.

**Alternative: Deep sedation with propofol ± remifentanil** could be considered if the IR team is comfortable with brief apneic pauses and the patient cooperates. However, GA with an LMA provides more reliable conditions.

### Induction

| Agent | Dose | Notes |
|-------|------|-------|
| **Propofol** | 1.5–2.5 mg/kg IV | Titrate to effect. Reduced protein binding in renal failure may increase free drug fraction. |
| **Fentanyl** | 1–2 mcg/kg IV | Preferred opioid — hepatically metabolized, no active renal metabolites. Avoid morphine (M6G accumulation in renal impairment). |
| **Lidocaine** | 1 mg/kg IV | To attenuate injection pain and smooth induction. |

If intubation required:
- **Rocuronium** 0.6 mg/kg (onset may be delayed in renal failure; duration not prolonged in children per Coté). Sugammadex available for reversal (2–4 mg/kg).
- **Cisatracurium** 0.1–0.15 mg/kg is an alternative — undergoes Hofmann elimination, independent of renal function.
- **Succinylcholine:** Avoid if K⁺ > 5.5 mEq/L. If K⁺ is normal, it can be used but expect ~0.5 mEq/L rise. Chronic hyperkalemia in renal patients is relatively well-tolerated, but acute rises are dangerous.

### Maintenance

| Option | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **TIVA** | Propofol 100–200 mcg/kg/min + remifentanil 0.05–0.1 mcg/kg/min. Remifentanil is ideal — ester hydrolysis, organ-independent elimination. Allows rapid titration for breath-holds. |
| **Volatile** | Sevoflurane via LMA (if anesthesia machine available in IR). Avoid prolonged high-flow sevoflurane due to theoretical compound A nephrotoxicity concern, though clinical evidence in renal transplant recipients has not shown negative outcomes (Coté 2025). |
| **Muscle relaxation** | Usually not required if LMA is used for a short IR procedure. If needed for absolute immobility, cisatracurium or rocuronium with sugammadex reversal. |

### Monitoring

| Monitor | Rationale |
|---------|-----------|
| Standard ASA monitors | SpO₂, ETCO₂, ECG, NIBP, temperature |
| **Arterial line** | Not routinely needed for a brief biopsy. Consider only if patient is hemodynamically labile or has difficult BP management. |
| **Two peripheral IVs** | At minimum one good-bore IV (18G or 20G). Avoid the arm with any AV fistula if present. Avoid lower extremities if possible (iliac vessel anastomoses). |
| **Foley** | Already in place (post-transplant). Monitor UOP as a marker of graft perfusion. |

### IV Access Considerations

**Critical:** In a renal transplant patient, vascular access must be planned carefully:
- **Avoid the arm with an AV fistula** (if one exists from prior dialysis) — no BP cuffs, no IVs, no blood draws on that arm.
- **Avoid subclavian central lines** if possible — risk of subclavian vein stenosis compromising future fistula options.
- Check which arm has a PICC or central line from the transplant admission and use it.

### Positioning

- **Supine** with the right side slightly exposed for the IR team to access the transplant kidney (which is in the right iliac fossa, extraperitoneal).
- Ensure airway access is maintained under drapes.
- Padding and warming (forced-air warmer or blanket).

### Fluid Management

- **Isotonic crystalloid** (normal saline or PlasmaLyte). Avoid lactated Ringer's if K⁺ is elevated (contains 4 mEq/L K⁺).
- **Goal:** Euvolemia. Avoid hypovolemia — graft perfusion is paramount.
- Maintenance rate: ~80–100 mL/hr for this adolescent during a short procedure.
- Replace UOP mL for mL if the graft is producing significant urine (carry this practice from the transplant team's protocol).

### Temperature Management

- Adolescents in IR suites cool quickly — use forced-air warming, warm IV fluids.
- Hypothermia worsens coagulopathy — particularly important given the bleeding risk of this procedure.

## Drug Considerations in Renal Impairment

| Drug | Consideration |
|------|---------------|
| **Propofol** | Safe. Hepatic metabolism. Reduced protein binding → titrate carefully. |
| **Fentanyl** | Preferred opioid. Hepatic metabolism, inactive metabolites. |
| **Remifentanil** | Ideal — ester hydrolysis, organ-independent elimination. |
| **Morphine** | **AVOID** — morphine-6-glucuronide (active metabolite) accumulates in renal failure → prolonged sedation, respiratory depression. |
| **Meperidine** | **AVOID** — normeperidine accumulates → seizures. |
| **Cisatracurium** | Preferred NMB — Hofmann elimination, independent of renal function. |
| **Rocuronium** | Acceptable. Hepatic and renal elimination. Onset may be delayed. Duration not clinically prolonged in pediatric renal failure. Sugammadex available for reversal. |
| **Succinylcholine** | Use with caution — raises K⁺ by ~0.5 mEq/L. Contraindicated if K⁺ > 5.5. |
| **NSAIDs** | **AVOID** — nephrotoxic, impair renal perfusion, platelet inhibition. |
| **Midazolam** | Safe for anxiolysis — hepatic metabolism, but active metabolite (α-hydroxymidazolam) has some renal clearance → use reduced doses. |
| **Ondansetron** | Safe for antiemetics. 4 mg IV. |
| **Acetaminophen** | Safe. 15 mg/kg IV (max 1g). Good baseline analgesic. |
| **Dexamethasone** | Check with transplant team — usually acceptable as antiemetic (0.15 mg/kg, max 4 mg) but may interact with immunosuppression protocol. |

## Intraoperative Emergencies & Management

### 1. Hemorrhage Post-Biopsy

**The most feared complication.** Incidence of significant post-renal biopsy bleeding requiring intervention is ~1–6%.

**Signs:** Tachycardia, hypotension, falling Hgb, expanding hematoma on US, visible blood from drain site.

**Management:**
- Large-bore IV access (already placed)
- Volume resuscitation: crystalloid bolus 20 mL/kg, then pRBC if ongoing
- Transfuse to maintain Hgb > 7 g/dL (or higher if hemodynamically unstable)
- Notify IR for possible embolization
- DDAVP 0.3 mcg/kg IV if not already given
- TXA 15–20 mg/kg IV (max 1g) if bleeding is significant
- Cryoprecipitate if fibrinogen < 150 mg/dL
- Platelets if count < 50,000
- Consider activating MTP if massive hemorrhage
- Call surgical backup (transplant surgery) for possible surgical exploration
- Maintain normothermia — hypothermia worsens coagulopathy

### 2. Hyperkalemia

**Risk factors here:** Impaired graft, tacrolimus, Plavix held (not a factor), potential blood product transfusion, tissue trauma.

**ECG signs (progressive):** Peaked T waves → widened QRS → loss of P waves → sine wave → VF/asystole.

**Treatment ladder:**
1. **Calcium gluconate** 60 mg/kg IV (max 3g) slow push over 5 min — membrane stabilization (immediate onset, duration 30–60 min)
2. **Sodium bicarbonate** 1–2 mEq/kg IV — shifts K⁺ intracellularly
3. **Insulin** 0.1 units/kg IV + **Dextrose** D25W 2 mL/kg — shifts K⁺ intracellularly (onset 15–30 min)
4. **Albuterol** nebulized 2.5–5 mg — shifts K⁺ intracellularly
5. **Hyperventilation** — respiratory alkalosis shifts K⁺ intracellularly
6. **Kayexalate** 1 g/kg PO/PR — eliminates K⁺ (slow onset)
7. **Emergent dialysis** if refractory

### 3. Anaphylaxis

Risk of reaction to contrast (if used), antibiotics, or latex.

**Management:** Epinephrine 10 mcg/kg IV (0.01 mg/kg of 1:10,000), repeat q3–5 min. Volume bolus 20 mL/kg. Diphenhydramine 1 mg/kg IV. Methylprednisolone 2 mg/kg IV. Albuterol for bronchospasm.

### 4. Laryngospasm (on induction/emergence if using LMA)

- **Positive pressure ventilation** with 100% O₂
- **Jaw thrust** and **CPAP**
- If persistent: **Succinylcholine** 0.25–0.5 mg/kg IV (only if K⁺ is acceptable) or **Propofol** 0.5–1 mg/kg IV
- Prepare for intubation

### 5. Bronchospasm

- Deepen anesthesia
- Albuterol via circuit
- Epinephrine 1–5 mcg/kg IV if severe
- Ensure not a mechanical cause (kinked ETT, endobronchial intubation)

## Post-Procedure Plan

| Item | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Positioning** | Flat bed rest × 4–6 hours post-biopsy. |
| **Monitoring** | Vitals q15 min × 1 hr, then q30 min × 2 hrs, then q1 hr. |
| **Labs** | H/H at 4 hours post-biopsy (as ordered). BMP if any hemodynamic concerns. |
| **Pain management** | Acetaminophen 15 mg/kg IV/PO q6h. Fentanyl 0.5–1 mcg/kg IV PRN for breakthrough. **NO NSAIDs.** |
| **Antiemetics** | Ondansetron 0.15 mg/kg IV (max 4 mg) PRN. |
| **Resumption of meds** | Resume immunosuppression per transplant team. Resume diet when awake/tolerating. Plavix — discuss with IR/transplant team when to restart (typically 24–48 hrs post-procedure if no active bleeding). |
| **Watch for** | Gross hematuria, flank/biopsy site pain, tachycardia, hypotension, falling UOP (may indicate graft vessel compromise). |
